Welcome to Arca

Arca is a personal operating system for focusing your effort on the goals that actually matter, both work and personal.

Most days are consumed by reactive work: emails, meetings, logistics, other people’s priorities. This day-to-day has to happen — but left unchecked, it devours all the time you needed for the things that move the needle.

Arca gives you a system to protect time for high-value work, track whether you’re actually spending that time, and build a weekly rhythm of accountability to yourself.

How It Works

Arca is built on four principles:

1. Focus on one primary goal (PG). Instead of spreading effort across everything, pick the one goal where success would change everything else. Most goals fail not because of bad planning but because the day-to-day crowds them out. Narrowing your focus is the first discipline.

2. Track inputs, not just results. There are two kinds of measures. Results tell you whether you achieved the goal after the fact — you can’t control them directly. Inputs are the activities that predict the result: the deep-work sessions, the drafts written, the experiments run. Track what you can control this week.

3. Keep a visible scoreboard. If you can’t tell at a glance whether you’re winning or losing, you’ll lose focus. Arca is your scoreboard. It tracks your deep ratio — how much of your time goes to focused, high-value work versus shallow logistics — plus tasks closed, streaks, and weekly trends. All updated automatically as you work.

4. Review weekly. Once a week, look at your scoreboard. What worked? What didn’t? What will you commit to this week? The weekly review is what sustains the other three principles over time. Without it, goals drift.

Set Your Primary Goal

If you could only achieve one thing in the next 30–90 days that would make everything else easier or more meaningful — what would it be?

A good goal is specific (not vague), measurable (you’ll know when it’s done), and time-bound (has a deadline). Think of it as: "From X to Y by when."

Daily Life Practices

Not everything important is a work goal. These are recurring personal practices — the daily habits that keep you grounded. Frequency matters more than ambition. Set a minimum target you can actually hit every day.

Your First Tasks

Now break your goal into concrete tasks you can work on this week. Each maps to one of two types:

Strategic — tasks that directly advance your primary goal. These are your inputs: the work that predicts whether you’ll hit the target. Protect time for these.

Maintenance — everything else. Emails, admin, meetings, logistics. The day-to-day. It needs doing, but it shouldn’t crowd out strategic work. This is the Eisenhower principle: important work rarely feels urgent, and urgent work is rarely important.
